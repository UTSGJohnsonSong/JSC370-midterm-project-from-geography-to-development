1 Introduction

Why do countries differ so dramatically in wealth, urbanisation, and economic complexity? One influential line of argument holds that geography shapes these outcomes in fundamental ways: countries in temperate climates, with coastlines and navigable waterways, have historically developed more productive agriculture and more complex economies. The logic is intuitive, and there is genuine empirical support for a correlation between latitude and income at the country level.

However, at the same time, it is clear that geography is not the whole factor to influence all aspects of the countries. Singapore lies almost exactly on the equator. Botswana is landlocked and tropical and has still managed rapid economic growth. Many cold-climate countries remain poor. Geography may matter in the background, but differences in institutions, colonial legacies, and government policy seem to explain a large share of what latitude alone cannot.

This project aim to take a focused approach to look at a cross-section of countries in 2019. The analysis asks whether geographic conditions are related to the way economies are organised, for instance, how dependent they are on agriculture, how open they are to trade, and how urbanised their populations are. It then asks whether those structural features are related to differences in income across countries. The idea is that geography might be connected to income partly through these economic structures rather than through a direct effect. If that is true, the apparent relationship between geography and income should weaken once those structure variables are included in the regression. The results should therefore be interpreted as descriptive patterns rather than causal evidence.

Core Research Question

To what extent does geography relate to economic development across countries, and does this relationship run through differences in economic structure?

Subquestions

  1. Are geographic characteristics associated with economic structure across countries?
  2. Are economic structure variables associated with national income levels?
  3. Does the association between geography and income weaken after controlling for economic structure?

Conceptual Framework

Geographya Economic Structureb Developmentc
Latitude, Landlocked Agriculture share, Trade share, Urbanisation GDP per capita

a Geographic proxies for climate gradient and maritime trade access following Gallup, Sachs & Mellinger (1999) and Limão & Venables (2001). b Structural transformation indicators following Kuznets (1966) and the World Bank Development Indicators framework. c GDP per capita as the standard cross-country income measure; see Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2001) and Rodrik, Subramanian & Trebbi (2004) for context on income determinants.

Geographic variables used here are distance from the equator (a rough proxy for climate zone), landlocked status (a proxy for access to maritime trade), and a binary tropical indicator. Economic structure is measured using three World Bank indicators: agricultural value added as a share of GDP, trade as a share of GDP, and the urban population share.

2 Methods

2.1 Data Sources

Two publicly accessible APIs are used in this project. Neither requires an API key.

REST Countries API (https://restcountries.com/v3.1/all) provides country-level geographic metadata including country name, ISO3 code (cca3), latitude and longitude, world region, subregion, landlocked status, and population. This is the source for all geography-side variables.

World Bank Development Indicators API (https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/all/indicator/{code}) provides the annual economic indicators. The five series pulled for 2019 are:

Indicator Code Variable Description
NY.GDP.PCAP.CD gdp_per_capita GDP per capita (current USD)
NE.TRD.GNFS.ZS trade_share Trade (% of GDP)
SP.URB.TOTL.IN.ZS urbanization Urban population (% of total)
NV.AGR.TOTL.ZS agriculture_share Agriculture, forestry, fishing (% of GDP)
SP.POP.TOTL population Population total

2019 was chosen as the anchor year because it is the last full year before the pandemic, has broad World Bank coverage relative to more recent years, and avoids the large economic disruptions of 2020 or later that would complicate a cross-sectional interpretation.

2.2 Data Acquisition

All data are pulled programmatically at runtime using the requests library. The two sources are then merged using ISO3 three-letter country codes, which are available in both and serve as a reliable country identifier.

One practical issue on the World Bank side is that the API returns regional aggregates and income-group totals alongside actual country entries, and many of these carry three-letter codes that look like regular ISO3 codes. For example, HIC is the high-income group, WLD is the world total, and EAS is East Asia & Pacific. Filtering on code length alone is not enough to exclude these — the same problem came up in HW2, where a clean_wb_data() function handled it using a curated exclusion list sourced from the World Bank’s own /region, /incomelevel, and /lendingtype endpoints. The same comprehensive list is adopted here: 44 regional codes, 7 income-level codes, several lending-type codes, and 6 IDA/IBRD grouping codes are all excluded, in addition to a regex check requiring three uppercase ASCII letters.

2.3 Cleaning and Sample Construction

Once both sources are pulled, the following cleaning steps are applied before the analytic sample is finalised:

  1. REST Countries entries with non-standard ISO3 codes (length not equal to 3) are dropped.
  2. World Bank entries matching the aggregate-code list or failing the [A-Z]{3} format check are excluded.
  3. The two sources are merged on ISO3 using an inner join (keeping only countries present in both).
  4. Rows missing latitude are dropped, and any duplicate ISO3 entries are removed.
  5. All analytic columns are coerced to numeric; values that cannot be parsed become NaN.
  6. A set of plausibility checks is run to flag any out-of-range values.
  7. Missing values across the five core variables are tabulated before the final filter.
  8. Only complete cases on the five core variables are kept for the analytic sample: latitude, gdp_per_capita, trade_share, urbanization, agriculture_share.

2.4 Feature Engineering

Derived variable Definition

tropical 1 if abs(latitude) < 23.5, else 0
log_gdp log(gdp_per_capita) — natural log of GDP per capita

Population source: Population figures are taken from the World Bank (SP.POP.TOTL, 2019) where available. For territories that have a REST Countries entry but no World Bank population figure, the REST Countries population field is used as a fallback.

2.5 Analytical Strategy

The analysis moves through four main steps: checking the data structure and missingness, summarising the variables descriptively, exploring patterns visually, and then running formal statistical tests and regression models. For the regression part, I estimated four nested OLS models using heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, adding variables in sequence to see how the geography-income relationship changes as more controls are included. Crucially, the same complete-case sample is used for all four models so that any changes in coefficients reflect the specification, not a change in who is in the sample. All regression results should be read as partial associations, not causal estimates.

def sig_stars(p):
    """Return significance stars for a p-value."""
    if p < 0.001: return "***"
    if p < 0.01:  return "**"
    if p < 0.05:  return "*"
    return ""

def safe_pval(p):
    """Format p-value for display."""
    return "< 0.001" if p < 0.001 else f"= {p:.3f}"

def fmt_coef(coef, se, pval):
    """Format a regression cell as coef***(SE)."""
    return f"{coef:.3f}{sig_stars(pval)} ({se:.3f})"

def scatter_fit(ax, x_col, y_col, df, xlabel, ylabel, title):
    """Scatter with OLS trend line and Pearson r annotation, coloured by region."""
    sub = df[[x_col, y_col, "region"]].dropna()
    ax.scatter(sub[x_col], sub[y_col],
               c=sub["region"].map(REGION_COLORS).fillna("#999"),
               s=28, alpha=0.72, edgecolors="none")
    xv, yv = sub[x_col].values, sub[y_col].values
    slope, intercept = np.polyfit(xv, yv, 1)
    xr = np.linspace(xv.min(), xv.max(), 200)
    ax.plot(xr, slope * xr + intercept, color="black", lw=2, ls="--", alpha=0.7)
    r, p = stats.pearsonr(xv, yv)
    ax.text(0.97, 0.06, f"r = {r:.2f}  (p {safe_pval(p)})",
            transform=ax.transAxes, ha="right", fontsize=8.5)
    ax.set_xlabel(xlabel)
    ax.set_ylabel(ylabel)
    ax.set_title(title)

def fetch_rest_countries():
    """Pull geographic metadata from REST Countries API."""
    url = "https://restcountries.com/v3.1/all"
    try:
        r = requests.get(url,
            params={"fields": "name,cca3,latlng,region,subregion,landlocked,population"},
            timeout=30)
        r.raise_for_status()
    except requests.exceptions.RequestException as e:
        print(f"Error fetching REST Countries: {e}")
        return pd.DataFrame()
    rows = []
    for c in r.json():
        ll = c.get("latlng", [None, None])
        rows.append({
            "iso3":       c.get("cca3", ""),
            "country":    c.get("name", {}).get("common", ""),
            "latitude":   ll[0] if ll else None,
            "longitude":  ll[1] if len(ll) > 1 else None,
            "region":     c.get("region", ""),
            "subregion":  c.get("subregion", ""),
            "landlocked": int(c.get("landlocked", False)),
            "population": c.get("population"),
        })
    return (pd.DataFrame(rows)
              .query("iso3.str.len() == 3")
              .reset_index(drop=True))


def fetch_wb(indicator, year=YEAR):
    """Pull one World Bank indicator for all countries in a given year,
    excluding aggregate/regional codes."""
    url = f"https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/all/indicator/{indicator}"
    try:
        r = requests.get(url,
            params={"date": year, "format": "json", "per_page": 300},
            timeout=30)
        r.raise_for_status()
        data = r.json()
    except requests.exceptions.RequestException as e:
        print(f"  Error fetching {indicator}: {e}")
        return pd.DataFrame(columns=["iso3", "value"])
    if len(data) < 2 or not data[1]:
        return pd.DataFrame(columns=["iso3", "value"])
    records = []
    for e in data[1]:
        iso = e.get("countryiso3code", "")
        val = e.get("value")
        if val is None or not iso:
            continue
        if iso in AGGREGATES or not _ISO3_RE.match(iso):
            continue
        records.append({"iso3": iso, "value": val})
    return pd.DataFrame(records)

Model results:

M1 (baseline, R² = 0.299). The first model regresses log GDP per capita only on distance from the equator. The coefficient on distance from the equator is 0.045 (SE = 0.005, p < 0.001), which means that countries farther from the equator tend to have higher income levels on average in this sample. This is a fairly strong bivariate relationship: geography on its own already explains about 30 percent of the variation in log GDP per capita across the 173 countries. At the same time, this baseline result should be interpreted carefully. Distance from the equator is standing in for many broader differences across countries, so M1 is useful mainly as a starting point rather than as a complete explanation.

M2 (adding landlocked status, R² = 0.394). Once landlocked status is added, the model fit improves noticeably, with R² rising from 0.299 to 0.394. The coefficient on distance from the equator increases slightly from 0.045 to 0.048 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), while the coefficient on landlocked status is -1.084 (SE = 0.193, p < 0.001). This suggests that, holding latitude constant, landlocked countries are associated with substantially lower income levels. The fact that the latitude coefficient changes very little after landlocked status is introduced suggests that these two geography variables are capturing somewhat different aspects of cross-country variation. Put differently, being farther from the equator and being landlocked do not appear to be telling exactly the same story.

M3 (adding economic structure, R² = 0.762). This is the key model in the sequence. Once agriculture share, trade share, and urbanisation are added together, the explanatory power of the model increases sharply: R² rises from 0.394 in M2 to 0.762 in M3. That is a very large jump, and it shows that economic structure is much more closely tied to income differences than geography alone. At the same time, the coefficient on distance from the equator falls from 0.048 in M2 to 0.022 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001). Relative to the baseline model, that is roughly a 51 percent reduction. The landlocked coefficient also becomes much smaller in absolute value, dropping from -1.084 to -0.425 (SE = 0.153, p < 0.01). This is one of the clearest results in the paper: once differences in economic structure are taken into account, the geography-income relationship becomes much weaker.

The structure variables themselves are also informative. Agriculture share has a coefficient of -0.066 (SE = 0.009, p < 0.001), indicating that countries with larger agricultural sectors tend to have substantially lower income levels. Urbanisation has a coefficient of 0.017 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), showing a strong positive association with income. Trade share is positive as well, at 0.003 (SE = 0.001, p < 0.05), although its coefficient is much smaller and less precisely estimated than the other two structure variables. Taken together, these results fit the broader pattern already visible in the descriptive plots: higher-income countries tend to be more urbanised and less dependent on agriculture.

M4 (adding log population, R² = 0.789). The final model adds log population as a size control. This increases R² further from 0.762 to 0.789, so country size contributes some additional explanatory power, but it does not alter the main story. The coefficient on distance from the equator remains very similar at 0.023 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), and the coefficient on landlocked status is -0.396 (SE = 0.145, p < 0.01). In other words, the weakening of the geography coefficients is not reversed once population is included.

The other coefficients are also fairly stable between M3 and M4. Agriculture share remains strongly negative at -0.066 (SE = 0.008, p < 0.001), and urbanisation remains strongly positive at 0.018 (SE = 0.003, p < 0.001). Trade share, however, becomes smaller and is no longer statistically significant in M4, with a coefficient of 0.001 (SE = 0.002). This suggests that the earlier trade result was not especially robust once population is taken into account. One plausible reason is that trade openness overlaps with other structural features in the model, especially urbanisation and sectoral composition, so its partial relationship with income becomes less stable in the fuller specification. Finally, log population enters negatively at -0.122 (SE = 0.029, p < 0.001), suggesting that, conditional on the other variables in the model, larger countries in this sample tend to have somewhat lower GDP per capita.

5 Summary

5.1 Main Findings

This project does not try to settle the long-standing debate about whether geography or institutions matter more for development. Instead, it looks at a narrower question: in a 2019 cross-section of 173 countries, how geography relates to economic structure, and how structure in turn relates to income.

A few patterns appear quite consistently in the data.

Geography and economic structure are related. Countries farther from the equator generally have smaller agricultural sectors and higher levels of urbanisation — the Pearson correlation between distance from the equator and agriculture share is −0.39, and between distance and urbanisation is positive and of similar magnitude. Landlocked countries also tend to be less integrated into global trade, a gap confirmed by the Welch t-test in Table 5. These patterns show up consistently across the scatter plots, correlation tests, and group comparisons.

Economic structure is strongly associated with income differences. Countries with large agricultural sectors tend to have lower income levels, while countries with higher urbanisation tend to have higher income. Trade openness is also positively related to income, although that relationship is somewhat weaker once the other structure variables are included. In the full model (M4), the three structure variables together account for a meaningful share of the cross-country income variation, with the R² rising from np.float64(0.299) in the baseline to np.float64(0.789) in M4.

The latitude-income relationship becomes noticeably smaller once economic structure is added. The distance-from-equator coefficient falls by roughly 51% between M1 and M4. This is the core pattern the project was designed to examine: part of what looks like a geography effect on income is actually reflecting underlying differences in economic structure. Geography still retains a statistically significant association with income in the full model, but its role is substantially reduced once structural differences between economies are taken into account.

## Introduction

Why do countries differ so dramatically in wealth, urbanisation, and economic complexity? 
One influential line of argument holds that geography shapes these outcomes in fundamental 
ways: countries in temperate climates, with coastlines and navigable waterways, have 
historically developed more productive agriculture and more complex economies. The logic is 
intuitive, and there is genuine empirical support for a correlation between latitude and 
income at the country level.

However, at the same time, it is clear that geography is not the whole factor to influence all 
aspects of the countries. Singapore lies almost exactly on the equator. Botswana is landlocked 
and tropical and has still managed rapid economic growth. Many cold-climate countries remain 
poor. Geography may matter in the background, but differences in institutions, colonial 
legacies, and government policy seem to explain a large share of what latitude alone cannot.

This project aim to take a focused approach to look at a cross-section of countries in 2019. 
The analysis asks whether geographic conditions are related to the way economies are organised, 
for instance, how dependent they are on agriculture, how open they are to trade, and how urbanised 
their populations are. It then asks whether those structural features are related to differences 
in income across countries. The idea is that geography might be connected to income partly through 
these economic structures rather than through a direct effect. If that is true, the apparent 
relationship between geography and income should weaken once those structure variables are included 
in the regression. The results should therefore be interpreted as descriptive patterns rather than 
causal evidence.

**Core Research Question**

> To what extent does geography relate to economic development across countries,
> and does this relationship run through differences in economic structure?

**Subquestions**

1. Are geographic characteristics associated with economic structure across countries?
2. Are economic structure variables associated with national income levels?
3. Does the association between geography and income weaken after controlling for
   economic structure?

**Conceptual Framework**

| Geography^a^ | Economic Structure^b^ | Development^c^ |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Latitude, Landlocked | Agriculture share, Trade share, Urbanisation | GDP per capita |

^a^ Geographic proxies for climate gradient and maritime trade access following
Gallup, Sachs & Mellinger (1999) and Limão & Venables (2001).
^b^ Structural transformation indicators following Kuznets (1966) and the
World Bank Development Indicators framework.
^c^ GDP per capita as the standard cross-country income measure; see Acemoglu,
Johnson & Robinson (2001) and Rodrik, Subramanian & Trebbi (2004) for context
on income determinants.

Geographic variables used here are distance from the equator (a rough proxy
for climate zone), landlocked status (a proxy for access to maritime trade), and
a binary tropical indicator. Economic structure is measured using three World Bank
indicators: agricultural value added as a share of GDP, trade as a share of GDP,
and the urban population share.

**Hypotheses**

| ID | Hypothesis |
|----|------------|
| H1a | Countries farther from the equator are expected to have lower agricultural dependence. |
| H1b | Countries farther from the equator are expected to have higher urbanisation. |
| H2 | Landlocked countries are expected to have lower trade openness than coastal countries. |
| H3 | Trade openness and urbanisation are expected to be positively associated with GDP per capita; agricultural dependence negatively. |
| H4 | The association between geography and GDP is expected to weaken after adding economic structure variables to the regression. |

## Methods

### Data Sources

Two publicly accessible APIs are used in this project. Neither requires an API key.

**REST Countries API** (`https://restcountries.com/v3.1/all`)
provides country-level geographic metadata including country name, ISO3 code
(`cca3`), latitude and longitude, world region, subregion, landlocked status,
and population. This is the source for all geography-side variables.

**World Bank Development Indicators API**
(`https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/all/indicator/{code}`)
provides the annual economic indicators. The five series pulled for 2019 are:

| Indicator Code | Variable | Description |
|----------------|----------|-------------|
| NY.GDP.PCAP.CD | `gdp_per_capita` | GDP per capita (current USD) |
| NE.TRD.GNFS.ZS | `trade_share` | Trade (% of GDP) |
| SP.URB.TOTL.IN.ZS | `urbanization` | Urban population (% of total) |
| NV.AGR.TOTL.ZS | `agriculture_share` | Agriculture, forestry, fishing (% of GDP) |
| SP.POP.TOTL | `population` | Population total |

2019 was chosen as the anchor year because it is the last full year before the
pandemic, has broad World Bank coverage relative to more recent years, and avoids
the large economic disruptions of 2020 or later that would complicate a cross-sectional
interpretation.

### Data Acquisition

All data are pulled programmatically at runtime using the `requests` library.
The two sources are then merged using ISO3 three-letter country codes, which
are available in both and serve as a reliable country identifier.

One practical issue on the World Bank side is that the API returns regional
aggregates and income-group totals alongside actual country entries, and many of
these carry three-letter codes that look like regular ISO3 codes. For example,
`HIC` is the high-income group, `WLD` is the world total, and `EAS` is East Asia
& Pacific. Filtering on code length alone is not enough to exclude these — the
same problem came up in HW2, where a `clean_wb_data()` function handled it using
a curated exclusion list sourced from the World Bank's own `/region`,
`/incomelevel`, and `/lendingtype` endpoints. The same comprehensive list is
adopted here: 44 regional codes, 7 income-level codes, several lending-type
codes, and 6 IDA/IBRD grouping codes are all excluded, in addition to a regex
check requiring three uppercase ASCII letters.

### Cleaning and Sample Construction

Once both sources are pulled, the following cleaning steps are applied before
the analytic sample is finalised:

1. REST Countries entries with non-standard ISO3 codes (length not equal to 3) are dropped.
2. World Bank entries matching the aggregate-code list or failing the `[A-Z]{3}` format check are excluded.
3. The two sources are merged on ISO3 using an inner join (keeping only countries present in both).
4. Rows missing `latitude` are dropped, and any duplicate ISO3 entries are removed.
5. All analytic columns are coerced to numeric; values that cannot be parsed become `NaN`.
6. A set of plausibility checks is run to flag any out-of-range values.
7. Missing values across the five core variables are tabulated before the final filter.
8. Only complete cases on the five core variables are kept for the analytic sample:
   `latitude`, `gdp_per_capita`, `trade_share`, `urbanization`, `agriculture_share`.

**Plausibility bounds:**

| Variable | Expected range |
|----------|----------------|
| `latitude` | [−90, 90] |
| `gdp_per_capita` | > 0 |
| `trade_share` | ≥ 0 |
| `urbanization` | [0, 100] |
| `agriculture_share` | [0, 100] |

### Feature Engineering

| Derived variable | Definition |
|-----------------|------------|
| `dist_equator` | `abs(latitude)` — distance from equator in degrees |
| `tropical` | 1 if `abs(latitude) < 23.5`, else 0 |
| `log_gdp` | `log(gdp_per_capita)` — natural log of GDP per capita |
| `log_population` | `log(population)` — natural log of population |
| `gdp_tercile` | Quantile-based income tercile (Low / Medium / High), used for visualisation only |

**Population source:** Population figures are taken from the World Bank
(`SP.POP.TOTL`, 2019) where available. For territories that have a REST Countries
entry but no World Bank population figure, the REST Countries population field is
used as a fallback.

### Analytical Strategy

The analysis moves through four main steps: checking the data structure and
missingness, summarising the variables descriptively, exploring patterns visually,
and then running formal statistical tests and regression models. For the regression
part, I estimated four nested OLS models using heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors,
adding variables in sequence to see how the geography-income relationship changes
as more controls are included. Crucially, the same complete-case sample is used
for all four models so that any changes in coefficients reflect the specification,
not a change in who is in the sample. All regression results should be read as
partial associations, not causal estimates.

def fetch_rest_countries():
    """Pull geographic metadata from REST Countries API."""
    url = "https://restcountries.com/v3.1/all"
    try:
        r = requests.get(url,
            params={"fields": "name,cca3,latlng,region,subregion,landlocked,population"},
            timeout=30)
        r.raise_for_status()
    except requests.exceptions.RequestException as e:
        print(f"Error fetching REST Countries: {e}")
        return pd.DataFrame()
    rows = []
    for c in r.json():
        ll = c.get("latlng", [None, None])
        rows.append({
            "iso3":       c.get("cca3", ""),

            "region":     c.get("region", ""),
            "subregion":  c.get("subregion", ""),
            "landlocked": int(c.get("landlocked", False)),
            "population": c.get("population"),
        })
    return (pd.DataFrame(rows)
              .query("iso3.str.len() == 3")
              .reset_index(drop=True))


def fetch_wb(indicator, year=YEAR):
    """Pull one World Bank indicator for all countries in a given year,
    excluding aggregate/regional codes."""
    url = f"https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/all/indicator/{indicator}"
    try:
        r = requests.get(url,
            params={"date": year, "format": "json", "per_page": 300},
            timeout=30)
        r.raise_for_status()
        data = r.json()
    except requests.exceptions.RequestException as e:
        print(f"  Error fetching {indicator}: {e}")
        return pd.DataFrame(columns=["iso3", "value"])
    if len(data) < 2 or not data[1]:
        return pd.DataFrame(columns=["iso3", "value"])
    records = []
    for e in data[1]:
        iso = e.get("countryiso3code", "")
        val = e.get("value")
        if val is None or not iso:
            continue
        if iso in AGGREGATES or not _ISO3_RE.match(iso):
            continue
        records.append({"iso3": iso, "value": val})
    return pd.DataFrame(records)

The correlation results in Table 2 are broadly consistent with the patterns observed in the scatterplots. Countries located farther from the equator tend to have smaller agricultural sectors and higher levels of urbanisation, and both correlations are statistically significant. The three structure variables are also significantly correlated with log GDP per capita in the expected directions. At the same time, these correlations reflect simple bivariate relationships and may capture broader regional or historical patterns associated with each variable, rather than direct effects on their own.

Before moving to the regression analysis, it is useful to examine how closely the three structure variables relate to one another. Table 3 reports the pairwise correlations among agriculture share, trade share, and urbanisation. The correlations are moderate but not extremely high. Table 4 confirms this using variance inflation factors (VIFs), all of which remain well below commonly used thresholds for concern. Taken together, these results suggest that including all three variables in the same regression model is unlikely to create serious multicollinearity problems.

The Welch t-test results in Table 5 compare landlocked and coastal countries. The results show that landlocked countries have significantly lower income levels on average, with the difference in log GDP per capita being statistically significant. In contrast, the difference in trade share between landlocked and coastal countries is not statistically significant. Although landlocked countries appear to have somewhat different trade patterns in the sample, the distributions overlap substantially. As with the earlier correlations, these comparisons should be interpreted cautiously, since landlocked countries also differ from coastal ones in other respects, including geography and regional context.


### Regression Analysis {#sec-regression}

To explore whether the relationship between geography and income changes once economic structure is taken into account, I try to estimate four nested OLS models and added variables sequentially. All models use the same complete-case sample, which means that any changes in the coefficients reflect the inclusion of additional variables rather than differences in the countries included in the sample. Robust standard errors are used to account for potential heteroskedasticity. Figure 10, shown after the coefficient table, illustrates how the two geography coefficients evolve as the model becomes more complex.

**Model results:**

M1 (baseline, R² = 0.299).
The first model regresses log GDP per capita only on distance from the equator. The coefficient on distance from the equator is 0.045 (SE = 0.005, p < 0.001), which means that countries farther from the equator tend to have higher income levels on average in this sample. This is a fairly strong bivariate relationship: geography on its own already explains about 30 percent of the variation in log GDP per capita across the 173 countries. At the same time, this baseline result should be interpreted carefully. Distance from the equator is standing in for many broader differences across countries, so M1 is useful mainly as a starting point rather than as a complete explanation.

M2 (adding landlocked status, R² = 0.394).
Once landlocked status is added, the model fit improves noticeably, with R² rising from 0.299 to 0.394. The coefficient on distance from the equator increases slightly from 0.045 to 0.048 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), while the coefficient on landlocked status is -1.084 (SE = 0.193, p < 0.001). This suggests that, holding latitude constant, landlocked countries are associated with substantially lower income levels. The fact that the latitude coefficient changes very little after landlocked status is introduced suggests that these two geography variables are capturing somewhat different aspects of cross-country variation. Put differently, being farther from the equator and being landlocked do not appear to be telling exactly the same story.

M3 (adding economic structure, R² = 0.762).
This is the key model in the sequence. Once agriculture share, trade share, and urbanisation are added together, the explanatory power of the model increases sharply: R² rises from 0.394 in M2 to 0.762 in M3. That is a very large jump, and it shows that economic structure is much more closely tied to income differences than geography alone. At the same time, the coefficient on distance from the equator falls from 0.048 in M2 to 0.022 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001). Relative to the baseline model, that is roughly a 51 percent reduction. The landlocked coefficient also becomes much smaller in absolute value, dropping from -1.084 to -0.425 (SE = 0.153, p < 0.01). This is one of the clearest results in the paper: once differences in economic structure are taken into account, the geography-income relationship becomes much weaker.

The structure variables themselves are also informative. Agriculture share has a coefficient of -0.066 (SE = 0.009, p < 0.001), indicating that countries with larger agricultural sectors tend to have substantially lower income levels. Urbanisation has a coefficient of 0.017 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), showing a strong positive association with income. Trade share is positive as well, at 0.003 (SE = 0.001, p < 0.05), although its coefficient is much smaller and less precisely estimated than the other two structure variables. Taken together, these results fit the broader pattern already visible in the descriptive plots: higher-income countries tend to be more urbanised and less dependent on agriculture.

M4 (adding log population, R² = 0.789).
The final model adds log population as a size control. This increases R² further from 0.762 to 0.789, so country size contributes some additional explanatory power, but it does not alter the main story. The coefficient on distance from the equator remains very similar at 0.023 (SE = 0.004, p < 0.001), and the coefficient on landlocked status is -0.396 (SE = 0.145, p < 0.01). In other words, the weakening of the geography coefficients is not reversed once population is included.

The other coefficients are also fairly stable between M3 and M4. Agriculture share remains strongly negative at -0.066 (SE = 0.008, p < 0.001), and urbanisation remains strongly positive at 0.018 (SE = 0.003, p < 0.001). Trade share, however, becomes smaller and is no longer statistically significant in M4, with a coefficient of 0.001 (SE = 0.002). This suggests that the earlier trade result was not especially robust once population is taken into account. One plausible reason is that trade openness overlaps with other structural features in the model, especially urbanisation and sectoral composition, so its partial relationship with income becomes less stable in the fuller specification. Finally, log population enters negatively at -0.122 (SE = 0.029, p < 0.001), suggesting that, conditional on the other variables in the model, larger countries in this sample tend to have somewhat lower GDP per capita.

profile** — Agrarian, Transitional, or Urban / Service-Oriented — using a

## Summary

### Main Findings

This project does not try to settle the long-standing debate about whether
geography or institutions matter more for development. Instead, it looks at a
narrower question: in a 2019 cross-section of `{python} n_an` countries, how
geography relates to economic structure, and how structure in turn relates to
income.

A few patterns appear quite consistently in the data.

**Geography and economic structure are related.** Countries farther from the
equator generally have smaller agricultural sectors and higher levels of
urbanisation — the Pearson correlation between distance from the equator and
agriculture share is −0.39, and between distance and urbanisation is positive
and of similar magnitude. Landlocked countries also tend to be less integrated
into global trade, a gap confirmed by the Welch t-test in Table 5. These
patterns show up consistently across the scatter plots, correlation tests, and
group comparisons.

**Economic structure is strongly associated with income differences.** Countries
with large agricultural sectors tend to have lower income levels, while
countries with higher urbanisation tend to have higher income. Trade openness is
also positively related to income, although that relationship is somewhat weaker
once the other structure variables are included. In the full model (M4), the
three structure variables together account for a meaningful share of the
cross-country income variation, with the R² rising from
`{python} m1_r2` in the baseline to `{python} m4_r2` in M4.

**The latitude-income relationship becomes noticeably smaller once economic
structure is added.** The distance-from-equator coefficient falls by roughly

### Limitations

Like most cross-country analyses, this study has several limitations worth
being explicit about.

| Limitation | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Cross-sectional design | Single year (2019); cannot capture long-run dynamics or establish temporal ordering |
| Omitted variable bias | Institutions, education, colonial history, and political stability are all absent from the models |
| No causal identification | All results are descriptive associations; regression coefficients should not be interpreted causally |
| Simplified geography proxies | `dist_equator` and `landlocked` are rough summaries of complex geographic conditions |
| Ecological fallacy | Country-level patterns may not hold at sub-national or individual levels |
| Sample restriction | Countries with missing data on any core variable were dropped, which may introduce mild selection bias |
